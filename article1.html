Why Most Tree Planting Projects Fail Before Planting Even Starts

Restoration 7 min read May 2026

The majority of tree planting projects in Kenya and across Africa fail not during the planting day, but weeks or months before — often before a single seedling reaches the field.

The visible planting event may look successful, but the real outcomes are determined much earlier through planning, logistics, nursery quality, and aftercare systems.

⚠️ Critical Reality:

High mortality rates are usually caused by weak systems, not by the planting event itself.

Weak Seedling Quality

Poor hardening, weak roots, and low-quality genetics reduce survival rates dramatically.

Poor Logistics

Delayed transport, poor coordination, and weak field planning affect planting success.

Weak Monitoring

Without aftercare and monitoring, even healthy seedlings struggle to survive.

The Hidden Pre-Planting Failure Points

Many organisations focus heavily on the planting event itself while neglecting upstream operational systems.

1. Seedling Quality Crisis

Many projects receive seedlings that are too young, root-bound, poorly hardened, or of unknown genetic origin.

2. Poor Planning & Timing

Ordering seedlings at the last minute, delayed site preparation, and lack of transport coordination often lead to planting during suboptimal windows.

3. Weak Monitoring & Aftercare

Without structured post-planting monitoring, replacement planting, and maintenance systems, even well-planted seedlings often die from weed competition, livestock damage, or drought stress.

Success in tree planting is determined long before the first hole is dug.
